Teaching Philosophy

I believe language learning is a deeply social process. When students interact with meaningful content and with each other, they develop not only language, but also key life skills—collaboration, problem‑solving, confidence, and empathy.

What I believe

Every lesson should be student‑centred. This means students do much of the talking—discussing, exploring ideas, and solving problems—while I act as a facilitator and guide.

I also believe the classroom should be a safe space, where every student can share ideas confidently without anxiety about making mistakes or offering non‑standard opinions.

How I put it into practice

1) Task‑Based Learning (TBLT)

Instead of teaching grammar rules first, I design group tasks that lead students toward the new topic. Students try to discover patterns and form rules together through collaboration. This keeps learning active and makes grammar meaningful.

2) Delayed feedback

When students speak, I avoid interrupting them to correct every mistake. During activities I note common errors, and at the end of the lesson we discuss them together. This helps learners reflect on their language, correct themselves, and become more independent.

3) Inclusive scaffolding

I support participation by providing scaffolds such as visuals, sentence starters, and useful phrases. This ensures that even students with lower proficiency can contribute to discussions and group tasks.

Where I am heading

These practices reflect how my MA TESOL studies are helping me move from traditional teacher‑centred instruction toward modern, interactive teaching. In today’s technology‑rich world, I aim to combine learner‑centred pedagogy with appropriate digital tools to make lessons engaging, inclusive, and effective.
